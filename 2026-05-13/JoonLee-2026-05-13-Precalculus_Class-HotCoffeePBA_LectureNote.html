📘 Notes — Newton's Law of Cooling Toolkit
1. The Main Formula
A hot (or cold) object placed in a room of constant temperature $T_{\text{room}}$ follows: $$T(t) \;=\; T_{\text{room}} \;+\; (T_0 - T_{\text{room}})\,e^{-kt}$$
  • $T(t)$ = temperature at time $t$ (in °F or °C)
  • $T_0$ = initial temperature (at $t = 0$)
  • $T_{\text{room}}$ = room (ambient) temperature — this is the horizontal asymptote
  • $k$ = cooling rate constant ($k > 0$; bigger $k$ = cools faster)
  • $t$ = time (minutes or hours — be consistent!)
2. The Horizontal Asymptote — Why It Matters
As $t \to \infty$, the term $e^{-kt} \to 0$, so: $$\lim_{t \to \infty} T(t) \;=\; T_{\text{room}} \;+\; (T_0 - T_{\text{room}}) \cdot 0 \;=\; T_{\text{room}}$$ The coffee can never get colder than room temperature. The graph starts at $T_0$, curves down (for hot coffee), and approaches $T_{\text{room}}$ — never touching it. On your PBA: the horizontal asymptote of the graph is the room temperature.
3. Solving for Time — "When does it reach $T_1$?"
Set $T(t) = T_1$ and solve for $t$: $$T_1 = T_{\text{room}} + (T_0 - T_{\text{room}})\,e^{-kt}$$ $$e^{-kt} = \frac{T_1 - T_{\text{room}}}{T_0 - T_{\text{room}}}$$ $$-kt = \ln\!\left(\frac{T_1 - T_{\text{room}}}{T_0 - T_{\text{room}}}\right)$$ $$\boxed{t \;=\; \frac{1}{k}\ln\!\left(\frac{T_0 - T_{\text{room}}}{T_1 - T_{\text{room}}}\right)}$$ (Flip the fraction inside to remove the negative sign — valid since $T_0 > T_1 > T_{\text{room}}$.)
4. Half-Temperature-Difference Time $t_{1/2}$
The time it takes for the difference $(T - T_{\text{room}})$ to be cut in half: $$t_{1/2} \;=\; \frac{\ln 2}{k} \;\approx\; \frac{0.693}{k}$$ Analogy to radioactive half-life — same math, different context.
5. Finding $k$ from Two Data Points
If you measure temperatures $T_1$ at $t_1$ and $T_2$ at $t_2$: $$e^{-k t_1} = \frac{T_1 - T_{\text{room}}}{T_0 - T_{\text{room}}}, \quad e^{-k t_2} = \frac{T_2 - T_{\text{room}}}{T_0 - T_{\text{room}}}$$ Divide: $\;\;e^{-k(t_2-t_1)} = \dfrac{T_2 - T_{\text{room}}}{T_1 - T_{\text{room}}}$ $$k = \frac{1}{t_1 - t_2}\ln\!\left(\frac{T_2 - T_{\text{room}}}{T_1 - T_{\text{room}}}\right) = \frac{1}{t_2 - t_1}\ln\!\left(\frac{T_1 - T_{\text{room}}}{T_2 - T_{\text{room}}}\right)$$
Typical values for context:
Room temp: $T_{\text{room}} \approx 68\text{–}72\,°\text{F}$  ·  Fresh-brewed coffee: $T_0 \approx 175\text{–}185\,°\text{F}$  ·  Drinkable: $T_1 \approx 130\text{–}140\,°\text{F}$
Ceramic mug: $k \approx 0.010\text{–}0.015/\text{min}$  ·  Paper cup: $k \approx 0.04\text{–}0.06/\text{min}$
Why is the horizontal asymptote exactly $T_{\text{room}}$?
The exponential factor $e^{-kt}$ approaches zero as $t \to \infty$ (because the exponent $-kt \to -\infty$). So the whole $(T_0 - T_{\text{room}})\,e^{-kt}$ piece vanishes — what's left is just $T_{\text{room}}$. Geometrically: the graph is the parent curve $e^{-kt}$ scaled and shifted up by $T_{\text{room}}$. The "floor" moves from 0 to $T_{\text{room}}$. That shift is the asymptote.
Worked Examples
Example 1 — Predict temperature at a given time
A freshly poured mug of coffee starts at $T_0 = 180\,°\text{F}$. The room temperature is $T_{\text{room}} = 72\,°\text{F}$. The cooling constant is $k = 0.025\,\text{min}^{-1}$. What is the temperature after 30 minutes?

Setup. We know $T_0 = 180$, $T_{\text{room}} = 72$, $k = 0.025$, $t = 30$.

Plug in. $T(30) = 72 + (180 - 72)\,e^{-0.025 \times 30}$
$\phantom{T(30)} = 72 + 108\,e^{-0.75}$
$\phantom{T(30)} = 72 + 108 \times 0.4724$
$\phantom{T(30)} = 72 + 51.02 \approx 123.0\,°\text{F}.$

Answer. $T(30) \approx 123\,°\text{F}$

Sanity check. After 30 min the coffee went from 180 °F down to ~123 °F — cooled by 57 °F. Still hotter than room temp (72 °F). Makes sense.

Example 2 — Find $k$ from two measurements
You measure a cup of coffee at $t = 0$ min: $T_0 = 175\,°\text{F}$. At $t = 10$ min: $T = 155\,°\text{F}$. At $t = 20$ min: $T = 138\,°\text{F}$. Room temperature is $T_{\text{room}} = 70\,°\text{F}$. Find the cooling constant $k$.

Strategy. Use the formula at $t = 10$:

$155 = 70 + (175 - 70)\,e^{-k \cdot 10}$

$155 - 70 = 105\,e^{-10k} \;\Rightarrow\; 85 = 105\,e^{-10k}$

$e^{-10k} = \dfrac{85}{105} = 0.8095$

$-10k = \ln(0.8095) = -0.2113 \;\Rightarrow\; k = 0.02113\,\text{min}^{-1}.$

Verify at $t = 20$. $T(20) = 70 + 105\,e^{-0.02113 \times 20} = 70 + 105 \times e^{-0.4226} = 70 + 105 \times 0.6551 = 70 + 68.8 \approx 138.8\,°\text{F}.$ Matches the given $138\,°\text{F}$.

Answer. $k \approx 0.0211\,\text{min}^{-1}$

Example 3 — Solve for time
Coffee starts at $T_0 = 180\,°\text{F}$. Room is $72\,°\text{F}$. The cooling constant $k = 0.025\,\text{min}^{-1}$. How long until the coffee cools to a drinkable $140\,°\text{F}$?

Set up the equation. $140 = 72 + (180 - 72)\,e^{-0.025t}$

$140 - 72 = 108\,e^{-0.025t} \;\Rightarrow\; 68 = 108\,e^{-0.025t}$

$e^{-0.025t} = \dfrac{68}{108} = 0.6296$

$-0.025t = \ln(0.6296) = -0.4626$

$t = \dfrac{0.4626}{0.025} = 18.5\,\text{min}.$

Or using the closed-form: $\;t = \dfrac{1}{0.025}\ln\!\!\left(\dfrac{180-72}{140-72}\right) = 40\,\ln\!\!\left(\dfrac{108}{68}\right) = 40\,\ln(1.5882) \approx 40 \times 0.4626 = 18.5\,\text{min}.$

Answer. $t \approx 18.5$ minutes

Practice Problems
Problem 1 Temperature after time — ceramic mug
Statement
A ceramic mug of coffee starts at $T_0 = 185\,°\text{F}$ in a room at $T_{\text{room}} = 68\,°\text{F}$. The cooling constant for this mug is $k = 0.018\,\text{min}^{-1}$.
  1. Write the complete equation $T(t)$ for this situation.
  2. What is the temperature after 15 minutes?
  3. What is the horizontal asymptote? What does it represent physically?
Solution
(a) Equation. $T(t) = 68 + (185 - 68)\,e^{-0.018t} = 68 + 117\,e^{-0.018t}$
$T(t) = 68 + 117e^{-0.018t}$
(b) At $t = 15$. $T(15) = 68 + 117\,e^{-0.018 \times 15} = 68 + 117\,e^{-0.27}$
$e^{-0.27} \approx 0.7634$
$T(15) = 68 + 117 \times 0.7634 = 68 + 89.3 \approx 157.3\,°\text{F}$
$T(15) \approx 157\,°\text{F}$
(c) Horizontal asymptote. As $t \to \infty$, $e^{-0.018t} \to 0$, so $T \to 68$.
Horizontal asymptote: $T = 68\,°\text{F}$ = room temperature
Physical meaning: the coffee will never drop below 68 °F (room temp). It asymptotically approaches but never crosses.
Problem 2 Find $k$ from one measurement
Statement
A cup of coffee starts at $T_0 = 180\,°\text{F}$ in a $70\,°\text{F}$ room. After 20 minutes, the coffee is $130\,°\text{F}$.
  1. Find the cooling constant $k$.
  2. Write the equation $T(t)$.
Solution
(a) Solve for $k$. At $t = 20$: $\;130 = 70 + (180 - 70)\,e^{-20k}$
$60 = 110\,e^{-20k}$
$e^{-20k} = \dfrac{60}{110} = 0.5\overline{45}$
$-20k = \ln(60/110) = \ln(6/11) \approx -0.6061$
$k = \dfrac{0.6061}{20} \approx 0.0303\,\text{min}^{-1}$
$k \approx 0.0303\,\text{min}^{-1}$
(b) Full equation.
$T(t) = 70 + 110\,e^{-0.0303t}$
Problem 3 When does it reach drinking temperature?
Statement
Coffee starts at $180\,°\text{F}$ in a $72\,°\text{F}$ room with $k = 0.030\,\text{min}^{-1}$. You like to drink coffee at exactly $140\,°\text{F}$.
  1. How long do you have to wait?
  2. Find $t_{1/2}$ (the half-temperature-difference time).
Solution
(a) Solve for $t$. $140 = 72 + (180 - 72)\,e^{-0.030t}$
$68 = 108\,e^{-0.030t}$
$e^{-0.030t} = \dfrac{68}{108} \approx 0.6296$
$-0.030t = \ln(0.6296) \approx -0.4626$
$t = \dfrac{0.4626}{0.030} \approx 15.4\,\text{min}$
$t \approx 15.4$ minutes
(b) Half-temperature-difference time. $t_{1/2} = \dfrac{\ln 2}{k} = \dfrac{0.6931}{0.030} \approx 23.1\,\text{min}$
Meaning: at $t = 23.1$ min the temperature difference from room is cut in half.
Check: difference starts at $180 - 72 = 108$ °F. Half = 54 °F. So $T = 72 + 54 = 126\,°\text{F}$.
$t_{1/2} \approx 23.1$ minutes
Problem 4 Identify room temperature from a graph
Statement
A cooling curve is given by $T(t) = 65 + 120\,e^{-0.022t}$.
  1. What is the room temperature (horizontal asymptote)?
  2. What was the initial temperature $T_0$?
  3. What is the temperature at $t = 60$ minutes?
  4. At what time does the coffee reach $90\,°\text{F}$?
Solution
(a) Horizontal asymptote. As $e^{-0.022t} \to 0$: $T \to 65$.
$T_{\text{room}} = 65\,°\text{F}$
(b) Initial temperature. $T(0) = 65 + 120\,e^0 = 65 + 120 = 185$.
$T_0 = 185\,°\text{F}$
(c) At $t = 60$. $T(60) = 65 + 120\,e^{-0.022 \times 60} = 65 + 120\,e^{-1.32}$
$e^{-1.32} \approx 0.2671$
$T(60) = 65 + 120 \times 0.2671 = 65 + 32.05 \approx 97.1\,°\text{F}$
$T(60) \approx 97\,°\text{F}$
(d) When does $T = 90$? $90 = 65 + 120\,e^{-0.022t}$
$25 = 120\,e^{-0.022t} \;\Rightarrow\; e^{-0.022t} = 25/120 = 0.2083$
$-0.022t = \ln(0.2083) \approx -1.5686$
$t \approx 71.3\,\text{min}$
$t \approx 71.3$ minutes
Problem 5 Two-point data fit + prediction
Statement
A scientist measures cooling data in a $68\,°\text{F}$ lab:
  • At $t = 5$ min: $T = 160\,°\text{F}$
  • At $t = 15$ min: $T = 130\,°\text{F}$
  1. Find the initial temperature $T_0$ and the cooling constant $k$. (Hint: set up two equations and divide to eliminate $T_0$.)
  2. Predict the temperature at $t = 40$ min.
  3. When will the object be within $5\,°\text{F}$ of room temperature?
Solution
(a) Set up equations. Let $A = T_0 - 68$. Then $T(t) = 68 + A\,e^{-kt}$.
At $t = 5$: $\;160 - 68 = 92 = A\,e^{-5k}$    ...(1)
At $t = 15$: $\;130 - 68 = 62 = A\,e^{-15k}$    ...(2)
Divide (2) ÷ (1): $\;\dfrac{62}{92} = \dfrac{A\,e^{-15k}}{A\,e^{-5k}} = e^{-10k}$
$e^{-10k} = 0.6739 \;\Rightarrow\; -10k = \ln(0.6739) = -0.3941 \;\Rightarrow\; k \approx 0.03941$
Find $A$ from (1): $\;A = 92\,/\,e^{-5 \times 0.03941} = 92\,/\,e^{-0.1971} = 92\,/\,0.8211 \approx 112.0$
So $T_0 = 68 + 112 = 180\,°\text{F}$.
$T_0 \approx 180\,°\text{F}$, $k \approx 0.0394\,\text{min}^{-1}$
Full equation: $T(t) = 68 + 112\,e^{-0.0394t}$
(b) At $t = 40$. $T(40) = 68 + 112\,e^{-0.0394 \times 40} = 68 + 112\,e^{-1.576}$
$e^{-1.576} \approx 0.2068$
$T(40) = 68 + 112 \times 0.2068 \approx 68 + 23.2 \approx 91.2\,°\text{F}$
$T(40) \approx 91\,°\text{F}$
(c) Within 5 °F of room ($T = 73\,°\text{F}$). $73 = 68 + 112\,e^{-0.0394t} \;\Rightarrow\; 5 = 112\,e^{-0.0394t}$
$e^{-0.0394t} = 5/112 = 0.04464$
$-0.0394t = \ln(0.04464) = -3.109 \;\Rightarrow\; t \approx 78.9\,\text{min}$
$t \approx 79$ minutes
Problem 6 Warming coffee — a reverse scenario
Statement
An iced drink starts at $T_0 = 38\,°\text{F}$ in a $75\,°\text{F}$ room. The same formula applies: $T(t) = T_{\text{room}} + (T_0 - T_{\text{room}})\,e^{-kt}$, but now $(T_0 - T_{\text{room}}) < 0$, so the drink warms. Given $k = 0.035\,\text{min}^{-1}$:
  1. What is the horizontal asymptote? What does it represent?
  2. After 20 minutes, what is the temperature of the drink?
  3. How long until the drink reaches $60\,°\text{F}$?
Solution
(a) Horizontal asymptote. As $t \to \infty$: $T \to T_{\text{room}} = 75\,°\text{F}$. The drink asymptotically warms toward room temperature.
Asymptote: $T = 75\,°\text{F}$
(b) At $t = 20$. $T(20) = 75 + (38 - 75)\,e^{-0.035 \times 20} = 75 + (-37)\,e^{-0.70}$
$e^{-0.70} \approx 0.4966$
$T(20) = 75 - 37 \times 0.4966 = 75 - 18.4 \approx 56.6\,°\text{F}$
$T(20) \approx 56.6\,°\text{F}$
(c) When $T = 60\,°\text{F}$. $60 = 75 + (-37)\,e^{-0.035t} \;\Rightarrow\; -15 = -37\,e^{-0.035t}$
$e^{-0.035t} = \dfrac{15}{37} = 0.4054$
$-0.035t = \ln(0.4054) = -0.9027$
$t = \dfrac{0.9027}{0.035} \approx 25.8\,\text{min}$
$t \approx 25.8$ minutes
Key insight. The same formula works for both cooling AND warming. The sign of $(T_0 - T_{\text{room}})$ determines the direction. The asymptote is always $T_{\text{room}}$ — the environment always wins.
